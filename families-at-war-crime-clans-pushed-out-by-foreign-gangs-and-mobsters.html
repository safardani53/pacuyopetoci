    Crime clans pushed out by foreign gangs and mobsters The Sun

    27 Feb 2024, 00:00

    THE Daniel, Lyons and McGovern crime clans are currently major forces in the underworld . . . but experts reckon they won’t be for much longer.

    We can reveal more than HALF of Scotland’s 238 gangs are family-based.

    But they are now being squeezed out by mobs who recruit members based on ability — and by major foreign gangs muscling in on their territories.

    In our guide to organised crime in the 21st century Investigations Editor
    RUSSELL FINDLAY looks at the families who’ve blighted communities — and how their days are numbered

    JUST like most of our family-owned corner shops before them, Scotland’s crime clans now face being put out of business by rivals who are bigger — and more ruthless.

    The small groups of relatives who have ruled their neighbourhoods by fear for decades are under pressure from all sides.

    In some cases they are competing against highly organised networks with international links who carry out smuggling and financial operations with
    all the efficiency of a supermarket chain.

    Another threat comes from foreign gangs embedded within the country’s melting pot of immigrant communities.

    Finally, the crime clans face being dismantled by the police — who find it simpler to work out what they’re doing precisely because of their high local profiles and close family connections.

    Leading criminologist Prof David Wilson, of Birmingham University, reckons these groups are more prominent in Scotland than the rest of the UK — but must adapt or die.

    He said: “The old family units are no longer truly the face of what we call organised crime. It’s much more flexible than that.

    “They may have their roots in the old clan system which evolved at first to provide protection and maximise profit.

    “But what we are seeing now is that those groups are definitely not as important as they might have been in the past.

    “You still see families involved in organised criminality with different members playing various roles.

    “But if they become notorious it is then much easier for the police to identify and target them.

    “It is no longer a desirable thing to establish a reputation like the Krays because all it does is attract the wrong kind of attention.”

    The late Glasgow ‘Godfather’ Arthur Thompson Snr dreamed of building a crime dynasty — but his offspring weren’t up to the task.

    Arthur Jnr was shot dead in 1991 — a crime for which former gangster Paul
    Ferris stood trial and was found not guilty..

    Another son, Billy, became a drug addict while his daughter Margaret died of a heroin overdose in 1987.

    A small number of Scots gangs — such as the Glasgow-based Daniel mob — have expanded beyond their traditional heartlands.

    Some have links to feared family groups south of the border, like the Adams and Arif families in London.

    The Adams clan has an estimated fortune of £200million and has been linked to 25 underworld slayings.

    But Detective Chief Superintendent John Cuddihy, boss of Police Scotland’s organised crime division, believes that small family gangs are vulnerable to major global organisations.

    And he reckons bigger gangster networks have the edge because they give jobs based on ability, not bloodlines.

    He said: “Organised crime groups at the higher end — ones that cross police force boundaries and operate internationally — are built on the same structure as a business.”

    Police Scotland’s organised crime experts reckon at least 25 foreign gangs operate here.

    They include Chinese Triads — who run cannabis farms and flog bootleg DVDs — people-traffickers from the Czech Republic and Bangladesh plus Caribbean
    Yardies who peddle crack cocaine and prostitutes.

    Senior detectives have also voiced their concern over a move by the ultra-violent Albanian Mafia into the vice and drugs markets in Scotland.

    In 2009 there were reckoned to be 367 serious organised crime groups in Scotland. Today there are only 238.

    That suggests a huge amount of “rationalisation” among gangs, with smaller ones being taken over.

    But Gordon Meldrum, who retired recently as organised crime director at the National Crime Agency, says we should never glamorise thugs.

    He explained: “Any view of these people as like something out of The Sopranoscan give a role model for young men to aspire to.

    “These criminals put pressure on good people every day. None should ever be glamorised and none are ever ‘untouchable’.

    “They should always be looking over their shoulder — they will never know when we’re going to put our hand on it.”

    Battling to stay at No1 - The Daniels

    THE feared family built a multi-million pound crime empire from the “jungle” in Glasgow’s Possil.

    They remain the country’s most powerful mob, despite being weakened by a feud with the Lyons and the ill-health of boss Jamie.

    His sister Annette Daniel, 52, was jailed last year for a £2,000 shoplifting spree and younger clan members have also been locked up.

    But the family still has its hand in drugs, metal theft, car crime, shoplifting and protection, as well as tobacco and booze smuggling.

    They also struck deals with smaller gangs for drugs and muscle.

    Sword bloodbath - The Malavins

    BROTHERS Zac, 21, and Angus, 32, hacked Andrew Curran to death in a park with a sword in 2010.

    The murder stemmed from a Curran relative urinating in Zac’s Buckfast. The 41-year-old victim’s friend James McGregor was also shot with a crossbow and
    suffered sword wounds.

    In 2014 another brother Kris, 27, was jailed for seven years for driving his car over McGregor, almost killing him.

    Shot dead at garage - The Lyons

    BOSS Eddie Lyons Snr ran a community centre with the backing of Glasgow City Council and the cops — until a bloody war erupted with the Daniel clan.

    In 2006 the hood’s nephew Michael, 21, was shot dead at a garage owned by his brother David near his base in the city’s Lambhill.

    Gunmen James McDonald and Raymond Anderson were jailed over the attack, which also wounded Robert Pickett and another nephew Stephen Lyons.

    The controversial Chirnsyde community centre was later closed and its former boss Eddie Snr and his clan were mostly driven out of Glasgow.

    But the family still have contacts in the Paisley underworld and with drug smugglers in Spain.

    Murder & betrayals - The McGoverns

    THE family rose to prominence in the 1990s as the self-styled McGovernment. A feud with lieutenant Jamie “Iceman” Stevenson resulted in the 2000 murder of
    Tony McGovern which remains unsolved.

    The outfit sided with gangland killer Robert “Birdman” O’Hara in a bid to tackle Stevenson.

    But it backfired when O’Hara and Iceman became pals.

    O’Hara was caged for life for the murder of rival Paul McDowall in 2005, while
    Stevenson served time for money laundering.

    The McGoverns still exert power in their heartland of Springburn, Glasgow.

    Caged for knifing pal - The Haldanes

    THEY started out as relatively unknown enforcers for the Daniels.

    But the family have since drifted away to carve out a reputation for drug dealing and violence in their native Possil, Glasgow.

    Mark Haldane, 30, is serving at least 16 years for fatally stabbing pal Mark
    Conway through the heart at his home in November 2010.

    His younger brother Duncan Haldane was just 16 when he was caught with £80,000 of heroin in 2008.

    Cops nabbed the teenager with the drugs stuffed down his trouser leg after tailing a minicab he was in. They then found a carrier bag packed with a kilo of smack.

    Tortured & burned - The Faulds

    BRIAN “Nightmare” Faulds was part of a violent gang in Glasgow’s Dalmarnock.

    The crook, 29, who once showed off his gun and knife wounds to a TV crew, was tortured, killed and burned in 2011.

    After his body was found brother Darren, 35, told the BBC he wasn’t thinking of “anything bar revenge obviously”.

    His father Ronald and uncles David and Robert have been linked to drug dealing and violence.

    History of violence - The Wingates

    THE family has long-standing links to violence and drug dealing in their native Paisley.

    David Wingate, 38 — once cleared of murder — was shot dead as he left a nightclub in the town in 1998.

    Three years later, William Wingate, 43, was jailed for seven years for stabbing a man to death in Glasgow.

    Last year, drug dealer Peter Wingate, 44, was caught with heroin, cocaine and a home-made blade in his prison cell.

    Yob links to terror - The Hamiltons

    THEY revel in ties to Loyalist organised crime groups in Northern Ireland and have preyed on residents in their Ayrshire patch for years.

    Boss Eric, 51, was jailed in 1998 after being caught with ammo and terrorist uniforms.

    Son William, 32, was locked up in 2007 for a machete attack. And another son
    Eric Jr, 30, centre — nicknamed Tyson — was caged in 2004 for drug dealing and being in possession of a shotgun.
